The Market for AI Infrastructure Is Larger Than Most People Think

Tyler Cowen · March 24, 2026 · marginalrevolution.com

There is a common assumption in the technology discourse that AI infrastructure is simply about training ever-larger language models and deploying them through APIs. This framing misses the far more interesting development happening at the margins: researchers and practitioners building coordination systems that turn individual AI capabilities into persistent, autonomous workflows.

Consider what this actually means. The difference between a tool you consult and a colleague who monitors, acts, and coordinates is not merely one of degree — it is a difference in kind. A tool requires you to formulate the question. A colleague notices when a question needs asking.

The Hayekian Parallel

The best way to understand these emerging systems is through the lens of distributed coordination theory. Hayek's insight about the price system — that it aggregates dispersed local knowledge into signals no central planner could compute — applies directly to multi-agent architectures. Each agent possesses local context (an inbox, a calendar, a codebase) that would overwhelm a monolithic system. The question is how they coordinate.

The curious task of economics is to demonstrate to men how little they really know about what they imagine they can design.

This is not merely an analogy. The mechanism design problems are formally identical: how do you create protocols that incentivize truthful information sharing between agents with different objectives and different information sets?

What This Means for Productivity Research

For those of us studying firm-level productivity, the implications are significant. We have excellent data on how firms adopt technologies — the Swedish employer-employee matched datasets, for instance, allow us to observe adoption, workforce composition changes, and productivity outcomes simultaneously. But the current wave is different from ERP adoption or cloud migration in a fundamental way: the technology is adaptive.

A firm deploying an AI coordination system does not merely automate a process. It creates a new information channel within the firm — one that learns, monitors, and acts. The measurement challenge here is non-trivial, and frankly most of the existing productivity literature is not equipped for it.

The bottom line: watch the infrastructure layer, not the model releases. The interesting economic effects will come from persistent AI systems that participate in organizational coordination, not from chatbots that answer questions marginally better than last quarter's version.
